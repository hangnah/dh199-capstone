What is "woman's work"?

In many cultures, for many centuries, it has included tasks like cleaning, sewing, and serving others. Women have done this work inside the home for their families, which may be why it has been taken for granted, dismissed as a “labor of love” that requires no other compensation. Some have not viewed it as “real work” at all.

Yet many women also clean, sew and serve people outside the home as janitors, garment workers and hotel workers. They labor alongside men in their workplaces and receive payment for it. In this way, they have redefined “women’s work” as a means to greater economic and social power.

But like many women workers around the world, female janitors, garment and hotel workers have not yet achieved equality in the workplace. They are not always compensated fairly for their labor, and their work does not always end when they get home. Often women must perform the same labor for their families as they do for their employers, blurring the boundary between workplace and home. As a result, their work is near constant but still undervalued.

The women of LA’s labor unions are fighting to assert the value of their work. They fight for equal pay, for better working conditions, for better childcare benefits. They fight to ensure that “women’s work” does not mean work that women have to do, but work that they are proud to do, that helps them to gain economic power, to provide for their families, to contribute to society in a meaningful way. Our project highlights working women’s fight for a better life – we hope you will join it.
